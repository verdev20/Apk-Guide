More Places Worth Visiting

Kota Beach

Considered by many the island's most picturesque beach, fronting Kota Beach Resort with fine white sand and a turquoise sandbar that appears at low tide, best seen in the summer months.

Santa Fe

Ogtong Cave Pool

A natural freshwater cave pool inside Ogtong Cave Resort, reached by a stone stairway down into a garden-lined sinkhole. Entrance fee for day visitors is roughly ₱100–200.

Santa Fe

Paradise Beach

A secluded, pristine stretch of sand reached by a short 10–15 minute trail off the main road — regarded by locals as one of the most beautiful beaches on the island.

Santa Fe

Virgin Island (Silion Island)

A boat trip northwest of the mainland, known for aquamarine water, wide white sand, coconut palms, and a cliff-jumping spot at its northwestern edge.

Island Hopping

Hilantagaan Island

The larger of two islets off the eastern coast, about a 20-minute boat ride away — home to a protected snorkeling area and a sandbar on its southwestern beach.

Island Hopping

Obo-ob Mangrove Eco Park

A community-managed mangrove forest explored by boat, kayak, or an elevated bamboo walkway. Entrance fee is around ₱50.

Bantayan Town

Kota Park (Spanish-era Fort Ruins)

Historic watchtower and fort ruins from the Spanish colonial period, sitting along the coastline of Madridejos with clear, shallow swimming water nearby.

A protected reef area off Santa Fe, popular for snorkeling trips arranged through local resorts and boat operators.

Eat & Drink

What to Eat in Bantayan

Bantayan's food scene is built around fresh seafood and one island specialty in particular.

Danggit (Dried Fish)

Bantayan is famous nationwide for its dried fish, especially danggit — sold fresh at the public market and often brought home as pasalubong.

SuTuKil Seafood

A Visayan style of preparing fresh catch three ways: sugba (grilled), tuwa (boiled), and kilaw (raw, marinated in vinegar) — common at beachfront eateries.

Holy Week Lechon

During Holy Week, Bantayan is known as the "Lenten Capital of the Visayas," when locals prepare lechon, a tradition unusual for Lenten season elsewhere in the Philippines.

MJ Square Food Hub

The island's main strip of restaurants and bars in Santa Fe, offering everything from eat-all-you-can seafood to casual international fare.

Before You Go

Travel Tips

Best Time to Visit

Dry season, December to April, brings calm seas and clear skies. Holy Week (March or April) is the busiest week of the year on the island.

Getting Around

Tricycles and habal-habal (motorcycle taxis) are the main way to get around. Renting a motorbike, around ₱350–500 a day, is common for exploring all three towns.

Rainy Season

May to November brings the southwest monsoon, with rougher seas that can affect ferry schedules — build in a buffer day if traveling during this period.

Three Towns

Santa Fe is the tourism center near the port; Bantayan town is the cultural and historical heart; Madridejos in the north holds the Kota Park ruins.

Getting There & Around

Port, Airport & Transportation

Bantayan Island is reached by sea or air, both through the municipality of Santa Fe.

Santa Fe Port

The island's main seaport and gateway, receiving RoRo ferries from Hagnaya Port on mainland Cebu. A larger Santa Fe Port project, with a finger wharf, passenger terminal, and marina, has been announced by the Port of Cebu.

Bantayan Airport (RPSB)

A community airport in Santa Fe with a paved runway, served by small-aircraft flights connecting to Mactan-Cebu International Airport. It has no major commercial jet service and limited scheduled flights.

Getting Around the Island

Tricycles operate within each town, while habal-habal (motorcycle taxis) and rented motorbikes are the standard way to reach beaches and spots between the three municipalities.

    About This Guide

    Why Bantayan Island

    Bantayan Island sits off the northern tip of Cebu, across the Tañon Strait — a slower, quieter alternative to Cebu's busier tourist spots. Its three towns, Santa Fe, Bantayan, and Madridejos, each hold their own stretch of coastline, fishing villages, and a Spanish-era church at their center.

    This guide covers every popular spot on the island itself — no other destinations mixed in — along with a practical ferry schedule, food to look out for, travel tips, and simple planning tools, so a trip here can be arranged without digging through a dozen scattered blog posts.
