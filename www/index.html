Port & Airport

Santa Fe Port

The island's main seaport, receiving RoRo ferries from Hagnaya Port in San Remigio, Cebu.

Bantayan Airport (RPSB)

Small community airport in Santa Fe. Limited scheduled flights to Mactan-Cebu. Most visitors arrive by ferry.

Eat & Drink

What to Eat in Bantayan

Fresh seafood, island specialties, and a unique Lenten tradition.

Danggit — Dried Fish

Bantayan is island-wide famous for danggit — dried rabbitfish sold at the public market. The island's signature pasalubong.

Market Find

SuTuKil Seafood

Fresh catch prepared three Visayan ways — sugba (grilled), tuwa (boiled in broth), kilaw (raw, vinegar-marinated).

Beachfront Eateries

Holy Week Lechon

Known as "Lenten Capital of the Visayas." Locals roast whole lechon during Holy Week — a beloved tradition unique to the island.

Seasonal Tradition

MJ Square Food Hub

Santa Fe's main food strip — eat-all-you-can seafood, grilled fish, cocktails, and casual international fare. The social heart of tourist Bantayan.

Before You Go

Travel Tips

Tap each tip to expand.

Dry season from December to April brings calm seas, clear skies, and the best swimming conditions. Holy Week (March or April) is the island's busiest period — book accommodation weeks in advance. The island is quieter and more affordable outside of Holy Week.

Tricycles operate within each town. Habal-habal (motorcycle taxis) cover beach-to-beach routes. For maximum freedom, rent a motorbike — ₱350 to ₱500 per day — and explore all three towns at your own pace. The main road runs north-south connecting Santa Fe, Bantayan town, and Madridejos.

May through November is the wet season. Seas can become rough and ferry schedules may be disrupted. Always build in an extra buffer day — you may find yourself stranded due to cancelled crossings.

Santa Fe — The tourism gateway. Ferry port, most resorts, beach bars, and restaurants are here. Bantayan Town — Cultural heart: oldest church, public market, mangrove park. Madridejos — The north's quiet edge: Kota Park fort ruins, Lawis Bay shellfish flats, and a slower pace.

Island Guide

Everything About Bantayan

Tap any section to expand.

Bantayan Island sits off the northern tip of Cebu, across the Tañon Strait — a quieter, slower alternative to Cebu's more commercial tourist spots. Its three municipalities, Santa Fe, Bantayan, and Madridejos, each hold their own stretch of coastline, fishing villages, and a Spanish-era church at their center.

The island is particularly known for its white sand beaches, crystal-clear shallow water, island hopping routes, and the unique Holy Week lechon tradition. It gained wider attention after Typhoon Yolanda (Hainan) in 2013 and has since rebuilt its tourism infrastructure while retaining its unhurried character.

Santa Fe — The tourism gateway. The ferry port arrives here. Most resorts, beach bars, and restaurants are here. Kota Beach, Ogtong Cave Pool, and MJ Square are all in Santa Fe.

Bantayan Town — The cultural and historical center. Home to the centuries-old Saints Peter and Paul Church, the public market, Obo-ob Mangrove Eco Park, and Tablayan Beach.

Madridejos — The north's quiet edge. Kota Park holds ruins of a Spanish-era fort and watchtower. Lawis Bay is known for its shellfish-picking tidal flats. The pace is slower and more local.

Wilderness Area status: Bantayan Island was declared a wilderness area under Presidential Proclamation No. 2151 (Dec. 29, 1981), later modified by Proclamation No. 902 (1996). This history is why parts of the island have historically had restricted land classification; national legislation has since moved to lift this "wilderness" tag to allow regular land titling.

Coastal & marine protection: Marine sanctuaries such as the Santa Fe Marine Sanctuary are protected under the Philippine Fisheries Code (RA 8550, as amended by RA 10654) and municipal fishery ordinances — spearfishing, dynamite/cyanide fishing, and taking coral or shells from sanctuary zones are prohibited.

Wildlife protection: The Wildlife Resources Conservation and Protection Act (RA 9147) prohibits harming or collecting protected marine species (e.g., turtles, certain shells) found around the island's reefs and mangroves.

Solid waste & single-use plastic: Under the Ecological Solid Waste Management Act (RA 9003) and local municipal ordinances (mirroring province-wide plastic-reduction measures in Cebu), single-use plastic bags and littering on beaches are restricted and may carry fines.

Local government authority: Santa Fe, Bantayan, and Madridejos each operate under the Local Government Code of 1991 (RA 7160), which is why each municipality issues its own tourism, business-permit, and environmental ordinances — always check for posted local rules at beaches, resorts, and heritage sites like Kota Park and the Bantayan Church.

Use reef-safe sunscreen at all marine areas. Carry your own reusable bags and water bottles — single-use plastic is increasingly restricted across Cebu's islands. Do not remove coral, shells, or any marine life. Treat shellfish-picking sites at Lawis Bay with respect — this is a daily livelihood, not a tourist attraction. Dispose of all waste properly; littering on beaches carries fines.
